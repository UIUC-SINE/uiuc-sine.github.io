Goal

The discussions focus on applications to numerical integration over a k-dimensional hypercube, i.e.

I=∫01dx1...∫01dxkf(c1,...,xk).

Remember that such a problem is incredibly general, and that many of our common sampling techniques are in fact solving this same problem.

Two major approaches to this type of problem are grid-based methods or sampling methods. Grid-based methods approach the problem through the perspective of Riemann integration. Under certain conditions, even simple grid-based methods can be incredibly fast.

Consider, for instance, integrating a truncated Gaussian using the trapezoidal rule. By the standard results commonly given, we would expect the trapezoidal rule to converge as O(N−2), while the Monte Carlo method should converge as O(N−1/2).

Let's look at how this works in practice.

So, clearly under some conditions, mandating even spacing is much, much faster than random sampling. As a side note, this is because the trapezoidal rule actually shares its convergence rate with the Fourier series, so when the Fourier series converges quickly, so does the trapezoidal rule [1].

This is just a specific example of the well-known idea that if you include extra structure, then you can often solve problems in a much more efficient manner. In Monte Carlo methods, extra structure is typically introduced through importance sampling by roughly matching the sampling distribution to the function magnitude.

Quasi-Monte Carlo

1.8 μs

Considering that we only use pseudo-random numbers for Monte Carlo integration anyway, this begs the question of if we can do better by putting more restrictions on the sequences with which we integrate, rather than trying to enforce that they look random.

Quasi-Monte Carlo methods take the point of view that Monte Carlo methods are a special case of a broader set of sampling based approaches to integration. These sequences are designed such that they satisfy the property that

1N∑i=1Nf(ai)→∫Qdxf(x)asN→∞,

where S={ai} is the sample sequence, and Q is the hypercube.

Clearly, by the law of large numbers, this holds for random sequences, but other sequences can be similarly designed, including the pseudo-random sequences commonly used. Rather than look at the convergence in a stochastic manner, consider the convergence of one realization of the sequence.

3.4 μs

In order to discuss more general sampling procedures, the notion of discrepancy is introduced in the following manner.

Let ν(x) be the count of the points of the sequence in the interval defined by x. Then the local discrepancy is

g(x)=ν(x)N−μ(x),

where μ is the measure of local region.

If we tile the space with intervals to generate a vector of local discrepancies, then we can consider norms over the space to be the distance between our sampling scheme and the true measure of the space. The norm using the supremum is denoted D(S), while when using L2 it is denoted T(S).

The idea is that convergence rates are bounded by the discrepancy of the sequence, i.e.

|∫Qf(x)dx−N−1∑k=1Nf(xk)|≤αfD(S),

where the coefficient is based on the total variation of the function. In dimensions greater than 1, the bound includes a discrepancy in that dimension, as well as in projections onto lower dimensions.

For this reason, sequences with rapidly decreasing discrepancy will converge quickly.

For random sequence, the notion of discrepancy is intrinsically tied to the variance of the random variables, and so the discrepancy is of O(N−1/2), as expected. There is a known lower-bound on the discrepancy of

D(S)≥csN−1(log⁡N)(s−1)/2,

which is less than that of a truly random sequence.

The known sequences tend to be around D(S)=csN−1(log⁡N)s−1, which is marginally faster than an actual Monte Carlo sequence.
